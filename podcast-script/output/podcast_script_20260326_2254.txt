Welcome to The Weekly Dose, your go-to podcast for the biggest stories making waves around the world. This week, we've got a doozy of a lineup: a war in the Middle East rattling everything from your gas prices to the stock market, the Olympics dropping a bombshell on gender policy, landmark rulings against Big Tech, and Netflix reaching deeper into your wallet yet again. Grab your headphones and settle in, because we've got a lot to cover.

Let's start with the story that's casting a shadow over just about everything else this week: the ongoing conflict between the United States and Iran. President Trump extended the deadline for striking Iranian energy sites by at least ten more days, saying talks are going "very well," though Iranian media has been far more skeptical. Markets clearly don't know what to believe. The S and P 500 posted its worst single-day loss since the war began, falling one point seven percent on Thursday as ceasefire doubts swirled. Gold whipsawed, dropping as much as three and a half percent before trimming losses. The dollar also slipped after Trump's announcement, while Brent crude climbed back above a hundred and eight dollars a barrel. Investors are caught in a painful cycle of hope and disappointment, and every presidential post on Truth Social is moving billions.

The economic ripple effects of this conflict are being felt far beyond Wall Street. Diesel prices in the United States have blown past five dollars a gallon for only the second time in history, and that matters because diesel powers virtually every industry, from farming to freight. US mortgage rates have jumped to six point three eight percent, the highest in six months, dampening what should be the busy spring housing season. Meanwhile in the Philippines, transport workers have gone on strike over skyrocketing fuel costs, with some drivers saying they're being choked by prices they simply can't afford. Across Africa, countries are rationing power and even diluting petrol just to keep things running. This is a truly global energy crisis, and ordinary people from Manila to Nairobi are bearing the brunt of it.

And this connects directly to what's happening in Norwegian politics this week. The Storting has been debating whether to cut fuel taxes, and the Centre Party crossed the aisle to join right-leaning parties in pushing through reductions in drivstoff, or fuel, levies, even though they weren't entirely happy about it. But economists are warning that putting money straight back in people's pockets could backfire by stoking inflation. According to E24, the bond market is now pricing in a possible interest rate hike in May, which would be a bitter pill for Norwegian homeowners who were dreaming of rate cuts. It's a classic policy dilemma: ease pain at the pump today or risk making everything more expensive tomorrow.

Shifting gears now to a decision that will reshape the world's biggest sporting event. The International Olympic Committee announced that from the 2028 Los Angeles Games onward, women's categories in Olympic sports will be restricted to biological females. The IOC says the policy will be enforced through what NRK describes as a "brutal" sex test, and the decision has already ignited fierce debate. Supporters argue it protects fair competition, while critics say it's dehumanizing and excludes transgender women who have competed for years. Meanwhile, India introduced a new bill this week seeking to change how transgender people are legally recognized and their right to self-identify, sparking protests across the country. These are deeply personal issues playing out on the largest possible stages, and the conversation is far from over.

Now to a landmark moment in tech accountability. A court ruled that Meta and YouTube must pay three million dollars to a woman who became addicted to their platforms as a child, marking the second child safety trial verdict to go against social media giants. Meta tried arguing that child exploitation on its apps was essentially "inevitable," a defense that did not go over well with the jury. The BBC's technology editor called it a potential game-changing moment for social media, suggesting it could be the beginning of the end for platforms as we know them. Meta has said it plans to appeal, but with more child safety trials on the horizon, the legal pressure is only mounting. For parents everywhere, this raises urgent questions, though experts say practical steps like setting screen time boundaries remain the best immediate tool.

Speaking of tech giants reaching into your wallet, Netflix confirmed another round of price hikes this week. The ad-supported tier climbs to eight ninety-nine a month, the standard plan without ads hits nineteen ninety-nine, and the premium tier now costs twenty-six ninety-nine. That's a two-dollar increase across the board on the higher plans. The Verge put it perfectly: death, taxes, and streaming services getting more expensive are the new certainties of life. With Disney Plus, Peacock, and practically every other service also raising prices in recent months, cord-cutting is starting to feel a lot less like the bargain it once was.

Sticking with tech, Wikipedia has decided to crack down on the use of AI-generated content in its articles. The online encyclopedia has been struggling with an influx of AI-written text that often sounds authoritative but can be riddled with subtle errors or outright fabrications. New policies are being put in place to flag and remove content that appears to be machine-generated. It's a fascinating tension: AI can write fluently enough to fool casual readers, but Wikipedia's commitment to accuracy means human editors are fighting back. Reddit is taking a different approach, announcing it will require suspicious-looking accounts to verify they're run by actual humans, though AI-generated content itself remains acceptable for now.

Meanwhile, Google is making aggressive moves across its AI portfolio. The company launched Search Live globally, a feature that lets you point your phone camera at objects and have a real-time back-and-forth conversation about what you see, in dozens of languages. Google also rolled out new Gemini tools that let users import their chat history and memory from competing AI chatbots, a clever play to lure people away from rivals like ChatGPT and Claude. And Google Translate's real-time headphone translation feature expanded to iOS and more countries, preserving each speaker's tone and cadence so conversations feel more natural. It's clear Google is betting big on making AI feel less like a tool and more like a seamless part of daily life.

On the Apple side, Bloomberg's Mark Gurman reports that iOS 27 will allow users to choose third-party AI chatbots to plug into Siri, meaning you could have Gemini or Claude answering your questions instead of Apple's own system. It's a notable shift for a company that has historically kept tight control over its ecosystem. But Apple also has security headaches: leaked hacking tools known as Coruna and DarkSword have put millions of older iPhones at risk of spyware attacks. While iOS 26 made strides in security, cybersecurity experts warn that anyone with an older device remains exposed. If you're still rocking an iPhone 11 or earlier, this might be the week to think seriously about upgrading.

Let's pivot to a story with real geopolitical weight. Ukraine's President Zelensky visited Saudi Arabia this week after offering to share Ukraine's formidable drone expertise. With the US increasingly consumed by its standoff with Iran, there's renewed urgency for Ukraine to find new partners and new deals to sustain its defense capabilities. This visit signals a pragmatic shift in Ukrainian diplomacy, reaching out to Gulf states that have maintained a complicated neutrality. Trump also confirmed that his delayed meeting with Chinese President Xi Jinping will happen in May, making it the first visit by a US president to China since 2017.

In a disturbing story out of Zimbabwe, the government revealed that at least fifteen of its citizens were killed after being lured to fight for Russia. Authorities say they are now engaging Moscow to repatriate the bodies and bring surviving Zimbabweans home. It's a grim reminder of how the ripple effects of conflict reach into countries far removed from the front lines, exploiting economic desperation to fill the ranks of foreign armies.

Closer to European shores, Hungary's ruling government under Viktor Orban is facing explosive allegations of mass voter intimidation ahead of upcoming elections. A documentary film features voters, mayors, and even a police officer alleging that money and drugs are being offered to pressure people into supporting Orban's Fidesz party. If substantiated, these allegations would represent one of the most serious threats to democratic integrity within the European Union.

In a story that connects trade and diplomacy, the European Parliament gave conditional approval to a new EU-US trade deal. Under the agreement, EU tariffs on American goods would drop to zero, while US tariffs on European imports would be set at fifteen percent. It's not exactly symmetrical, but supporters say it's the best deal Europe could get given Washington's current protectionist mood. The deal still needs final ratification, and plenty of European industries are watching nervously.

On the business front, French spirits giant Pernod Ricard is reportedly exploring a potential acquisition of Brown-Forman, the American company behind Jack Daniel's whiskey. According to Bloomberg, the move reflects a broader trend of consolidation in the alcoholic beverage industry, which has been struggling with declining consumer demand. If the deal goes through, it would create a global drinks powerhouse and mark one of the biggest transactions in the spirits sector in years.

Now for a story that's equal parts mystery and comedy. Norway's Ministry of Foreign Affairs discovered a key with a label that read "Rød-Larsen residence," referring to Terje Rød-Larsen, the controversial former diplomat. The problem? The four hundred and fifty kilogram safe it apparently belongs to has vanished without a trace. Nobody at the ministry can explain where a safe weighing nearly half a ton went, or what was inside it. It's the kind of story that sounds like it belongs in a Nordic noir novel, and speaking of which, actor Tobias Santelmann spoke this week about stepping into the shoes of Harry Hole, the beloved detective from Jo Nesbø's crime novels, saying his version will be "a bit warmer" than the character from the books.

And finally, some uplifting news from Norway's fjords. The first battery-powered tourist boat completed its maiden voyage in the Geirangerfjord this week, as new regulations mandate zero emissions from tourist boats and ferries in the UNESCO-protected area. The Geirangerfjord is one of Norway's most iconic natural landmarks, and this marks a genuine milestone in green tourism. It's a small boat making a big statement about what's possible when environmental policy meets innovation.

From the tremors of the Iran conflict shaking markets and fuel prices worldwide, to the Olympics redrawing the lines of inclusion, to AI reshaping everything from Wikipedia to your iPhone, it has been a week that reminded us just how interconnected our world truly is.
